üß† 0th Class: What is Algebra?

Exploring the idea of algebra, equivalence, sets, and foundational arithmetic in \(\mathbb{N}\).

Equivalence Relation

  • \(x \sim x\quad\forall x\in X\)
  • \(x \sim y \Rightarrow y \sim x\)
  • \(x \sim y\) and \(y \sim z \Rightarrow x \sim z\)

Equivalence class: \[ [x] = \{\,y\in X\mid x\sim y\} \]

Note: Either \([x]=[y]\) or \([x]\cap[y]=\phi\), so \(X\) is the disjoint union of its classes.

Check: If \(a\in[x]\) then \([x]=[a]\).

Successor Operation on Sets

Define \(S(A)=A\cup\{A\}\).

  • \(1\rightarrow S(\phi)=\{\phi\}\)
  • \(2\rightarrow S(\{\phi\})=\{\phi,\{\phi\}\}\)
  • \(3\rightarrow S(\{\phi,\{\phi\}\})=\{\phi,\{\phi\},\{\phi,\{\phi\}\}\}\)

If \(0\rightarrow\phi\), then:

  • \(1\rightarrow\{0\}\)
  • \(2\rightarrow\{0,1\}\)
  • \(3\rightarrow\{0,1,2\}\)
  • \(\mathbb{N}=\{0,1,2,3,\dots\}\)

Remark: Every natural number is the set of all its predecessors.

Peano‚Äôs Axioms & Arithmetic

Peano‚Äôs Axioms

  • 0 is a natural number.
  • Successor of a natural number is a natural number.
  • 0 is not the successor of any natural number.
  • \(S(n)=S(m)\Rightarrow n=m\)
  • Induction: True for 0 and true for \(n\Rightarrow\) true for \(S(n)\) ‚áí holds ‚àÄ\(n\).

Addition on \(\mathbb{N}\)

  • \(n+0=n\quad\forall n\in\mathbb{N}\)
  • \(n+S(m)=S(n+m)\) ‚Äî recursive

Example: \(n+1=n+S(0)=S(n)\).

Multiplication on \(\mathbb{N}\)

  • \(n\times0=0\quad\forall n\in\mathbb{N}\)
  • \(n\times S(m)=n\times m+n\)

Properties under \(+\)

  • \(n+(m+p)=(n+m)+p\)
  • \(n+0=0+n\) (monoid with axioms 1 & 2)
  • \(n+m=m+n\) (abelian monoid)
  • \(n+m=n+p\Rightarrow m=p\) (cancellative abelian monoid)

Embedding via Injection

Let \(X,Y\) be sets and \(\phi:X\to Y\) injective. Identify \(x\in X\) with \(\phi(x)\) to view \(X\subset Y\).

Equivalence Classes & Integers

Let \(X=\mathbb{N}\times\mathbb{N}\). Define \((a,b)\sim(a',b')\) iff \(a+b'=b+a'\).

Check: It‚Äôs an equivalence relation.

Classes \(Y=\{[(a,b)]\mid a,b\in\mathbb{N}\}\). Define \[ [(a,b)]+[(c,d)]=[(a+c,\;b+d)]. \]

Well-defined: if \([(a,b)]=[(a',b')]\) and \([(c,d)]=[(c',d')]\), then sums coincide.

Hence \(Y=\mathbb{Z}\). \((\mathbb{Z},+)\) is associative, has identity \([(0,0)]\), and is Abelian.

Remark: \([(a,a)]=[(0,0)]\).

Embed via \(\phi:\mathbb{N}\to\mathbb{Z},\ \phi(n)=[(n,0)]\). Then \(\phi\) is injective and \(\phi(a)+\phi(b)=\phi(a+b)\). We identify \(a\) with \([(a,0)]\) and write \(-a\) for \([(0,a)]\).

Construction of Rational Numbers

Let \(X=\mathbb{Z}\times(\mathbb{Z}\setminus\{0\})\). Define \((a,b)\sim(a',b')\) iff \(ab'=ba'\). Then \(\mathbb{Q}=\{[(a,b)]\}\) with \[ [(a,b)]+[(c,d)]=[(ad+bc,\;bd)]. \]

Check well‚Äëdefinedness similarly.

Multiplication on \(\mathbb{Q}\)

\[ [(a,b)]\cdot[(c,d)] = [(ac,\;bd)]. \]

Embed \(\phi:\mathbb{Z}\to\mathbb{Q},\ \phi(a)=[(a,1)]\). Then:

  • \(\phi\) is injective
  • \(\phi(a+b)=\phi(a)+\phi(b)\)
  • \(\phi(a\cdot b)=\phi(a)\cdot\phi(b)\)

Identity is \([(1,1)]\).

Claim

Every non‚Äëzero rational has a multiplicative inverse.

Proof

For \(a,b\neq0\), \[ [(a,b)]\cdot[(b,a)]=[(1,1)], \] so \([(b,a)]\) is the inverse.

Remark

Identify \(a\in\mathbb{Z}\) as \([(a,1)]\), write \([(1,b)]\) as \(\tfrac1b\). Then \[ [(m,n)] = [(m,1)]\cdot[(1,n)] = \tfrac mn. \]
